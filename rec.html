UC Recreation App Redesign: Streamlining Student Booking & Campus Access

Redesigning UC San Diego’s Recreation app to streamline the booking process and improve community access to fitness, wellness, and recreation activities on campus. This case study explores how an outdated app was reimagined into a functional, easily-accessible tool.

Organization

UC San Diego Recreation

Tools

Figma, FigJam

Project timeline

January – February 2025

Role

UI/UX designer

Design Opportunity

My inspiration for this project started when I wanted to book a yoga class through UC San Diego Recreation. Instead of a simple process, I found myself clicking in circles through a cluttered website that made even basic tasks feel confusing. That frustration made me wonder if this was just a UCSD issue or a larger problem across recreation platforms.

I began looking at other UC campuses–most of their websites were serviceable, though not always elegant. But I found a deeper issue with their mobile apps, where almost all buttons redirected me to the website, and the app did not serve a clear purpose.

To dig further, I spoke with UCSD students and faculty about their own experiences. The pattern was clear—while they valued Recreation as part of campus life, almost everyone avoided the app after a few attempts. Many described it as “unfinished,” while others simply deleted it. Even the website–which was functional–felt overwhelming and difficult on mobile.

I saw UC San Diego’s underperforming digital tools as an inconvenience to the organization’s ability to deliver on its mission: to engage the campus community in lifelong well-being and active living. If students can’t easily book a class, check schedules, or reserve a court, then the digital platform is failing to support the very values of inclusion, service, and community that UC Rec stands for. It became an opportunity for me to address this issue and connect my campus community to UC Rec’s thriving in-person programs.

Mapping the Current Experience

Refining The Flow

To move from research to design, I created an empathy map and user journeys to understand how different users, including students and faculty, interact with UC Recreation. From there, I developed personas and drafted multiple user flows to compare pain points in booking classes versus booking courts. I sketched low-fidelity wireframes of each option to visualize potential improvements.

A key trade-off was deciding which flow to prioritize. While faculty had valid needs, the interviews showed that undergraduates were the most frustrated with booking courts through the app. Since they also represented the majority of users, I chose to focus on this flow for the redesign. This decision allowed me to directly address the highest pain point with the clearest opportunity for improvement.

Court Booking Flow

The redesigned flow begins with a secure single sign-on using UCSD credentials. From the homepage, students can quickly select the type of court they want, view available time slots, and finalize a booking in just a few taps. They also have the flexibility to add their reservation to a calendar, modify details, or cancel if needed.

Usability Testing & Iteration

After building the high-fidelity prototype, I tested the flow with undergraduate students who regularly use UC Recreation. Their feedback gave me two key opportunities: adding reminder notifications before reservations and providing confirmation after check-in. I designed a notification system and added a QR-based check-in feature to close the loop, making the experience reliable and complete.

Reflections & Learnings

If I had more time, I would expand this project to include the user flow for booking fitness classes, making the app more accessible for a wider range of UC Recreation users. For this case study, I intentionally narrowed the scope to court booking, so I could deliver a high-fidelity prototype with a clear, reliable flow that minimized confusion and helped students reach their goal.

This project taught me how to prioritize impact, make trade-offs, and design with focus. I also strengthened my skills in Figma, especially in visual design and high-fidelity prototyping, while gaining confidence in applying user feedback to iterate on interaction design decisions.
