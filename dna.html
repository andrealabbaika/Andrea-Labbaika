DNA: Website Design for an IT Platform

Robot ICT is an IT automation company based in Prague, and DNA (Dynamic Network Automation) is its flagship product. DNA is a powerful platform that simplifies complex IT tasks for mid to large-sized companies, yet the company’s online presence didn’t reflect its strengths.

The original microsite was just a single page with minimal content, leaving the product underrepresented compared to global competitors. This project was about giving DNA a polished website that matched the quality of the product itself.

Company

Robot ICT

Tools

Figma, FigJam, Framer, Adobe XD

Project timeline

June – August 2025

Role

Web designer

Why the Microsite Fell Short

The challenge wasn’t with the product, but rather how it was being presented. The microsite lacked structure, clear CTAs, and a visual design that communicated credibility. Users couldn’t see DNA’s robustness or understand how it stood apart from competitors like Cisco or Juniper, which had highly polished and persuasive websites.

To address this, I conducted competitor analysis in the automation industry, a SWOT analysis for Robot ICT, and communicated with the team to follow their business priorities. DNA’s mission is to help organizations automate IT processes efficiently and securely, but the website wasn’t aligned with that mission. My goal was to narrow this gap by redesigning the site to highlight DNA’s features, reflect the company’s values, and guide users toward understanding the product.

New Design & Strategy

I restructured the site to focus on clarity and business goals. Low-fidelity prototypes were created in Figma, then implemented in Framer to refine the designs and publish the final website. The top navigation emphasized Free Trial and Self-Guided Demo, which were the two most important CTAs.

The landing page was redesigned to showcase DNA’s key features upfront, followed by tabs highlighting solutions for specific industries, integrations with tools, and detailed use cases. Instead of building an overwhelming number of pages, I narrowed the structure to a handful of essential ones: Product, Resources, and About Us. This made the site easy to navigate while still communicating necessary information.

Each page was designed to balance high-quality visuals with practical information, so that the new website not only looked professional but also supported DNA’s positioning against established competitors.

Landing page

Introduces DNA’s core value proposition, key features, and primary calls-to-action in a single, focused hero experience.

Free trial page

Guides prospective customers through a streamlined trial request with clear expectations, reducing friction before signup.

Self-guided tour page

Lets users explore DNA’s capabilities at their own pace, highlighting workflows, integrations, and real-world applications.

About DNA page

Builds credibility by positioning DNA within Robot ICT’s broader mission, values, and long-term vision for automation.

Final Results

By the end of the project, I had designed fully responsive screens for desktop, tablet, and mobile, making sure that every page felt consistent and polished. The new website transforms DNA’s presence from an underpowered microsite into a professional, structured platform with clear navigation and calls-to-action.

The redesign better communicates DNA’s strengths, creates a more compelling first impression for prospective clients, and helps position Robot ICT alongside global competitors in the IT automation space.

Takeaways & Next Steps

This project pushed me to strengthen my technical design skills, especially working in Figma and Framer, while also improving my competitor research abilities. At the same time, I recognized a limitation: without clearly defined user groups, I couldn’t conduct interviews or usability testing.

If I had more time and clarity on the audience—such as IT administrators in cloud services or telecom—I would have involved them in walkthroughs of the original microsite to note pain points, then iterated with usability studies on the redesigned flows.

This experience focused more on strengthening my visual and structural design skills, while reminding me how important it is to ground future projects in deeper user research so the final product truly reflects the needs of its primary users.
